# Claude Corps Fellow Application Questions

## Community Impact

Question: Tell us about something you've done that made a real difference for a community you're part of — a neighborhood, school, workplace, team, or any group you belong to. What was the problem, what did you do, and what drove you to put your time into it?

Limit: 350 words

Answer:

When I started the BGSU Developer Club, I kept meeting students who had an idea for an app or website but did not know what to do next. Some were overwhelmed by how quickly technology changes, while others simply did not have anyone to build with. I had felt that same way before, so I wanted to create the kind of community I wished I had when I was starting out.

Our goal was simple: help students stop thinking of themselves only as people who study technology and start seeing themselves as people who can build with it. We encouraged everyone to find a personal project they genuinely cared about and keep working on it, even if the first version was small or imperfect.

As founder and president, I organized weekly meetings where more than 15 active students talked about what they were building, shared problems they had run into, and helped each other figure out the next step. Sometimes that meant discussing which technology to use. Other times, it meant helping someone narrow an idea that had become too ambitious or encouraging them when they felt stuck.

What mattered most to me was creating a space where students could be honest about what they did not know. Nobody needed to be the most experienced person in the room. They just needed to be curious and willing to try.

My favorite moments were when someone made their first commit, deployed something they had built, or finally realized that their idea did not have to stay in a notes app. Seeing that confidence grow was the reason I kept putting time into the club.

## Setback and Personal Growth

Question: Tell us about a time something went wrong — a mistake, a setback, or a plan that didn't work out — and what you learned from it. Focus on what changed: how do you think or work differently now because of that experience?

Limit: 350 words

Answer:

For my capstone, my team set out to build an Ionic mobile app that could simulate population changes based on different demographic factors. It used a Node.js backend and a MySQL database, all running locally. We never published it, but our goal was to have a working prototype that we could demo with the calculations and simulation logic behaving correctly.

The project started with three people. One teammate stopped showing up, and the other moved away with about three weeks left. By the final two weeks, I was effectively working alone, and most of what we had did not fit together. The interface needed to be redesigned, the data model had problems, and the population calculations and movement logic were not producing reliable results.

I spoke with our professor, adjusted the requirements, and rebuilt most of the project. It was stressful, especially because I was rewriting major parts instead of polishing a finished application. Still, I managed to get the Ionic frontend, Node backend, MySQL database, and simulation logic working together well enough to demonstrate a functional prototype.

For a while, I thought the lesson was simply that I should not depend on other people. That was the easy conclusion, but it was not the honest one. I also needed to become a better leader. I had not given people clear enough tasks or created small checkpoints where we could catch problems earlier. I was also too focused on everything that remained unfinished. When someone made a small amount of progress, I often saw the backlog instead of recognizing the effort.

I think about that now whenever I lead a project. I define smaller pieces of work, check integration earlier, communicate risks sooner, and keep a reduced-scope plan ready. More personally, I try to be kinder and more patient. I am still learning how to hold people accountable while also recognizing progress, but this project changed what leadership means to me.
