# Cover Letter Guide

Use this guide when drafting cover letters for specific roles. The default style should be personal, technical, concise, and grounded in verified experience.

## Core Strategy

A strong cover letter should answer three questions:

- Why this company and role?
- Why Aryan's background fits the role's technical needs?
- What human context, judgment, or motivation does the resume not fully show?

Do not use the cover letter as a paragraph version of the resume. Use it to connect one or two high-signal technical examples to a real reason for the company, product, mission, domain, or team.

## Non-Negotiable Rules

- Ask personalization questions before drafting any cover letter.
- Keep the letter under one page.
- Use three to four short paragraphs.
- Tailor every letter to the company and job description.
- Use role keywords naturally when they are supported by Aryan's experience.
- Use confident, active language without exaggeration.
- Include a personal connection only when it is real and specific.
- Prefer one deep technical example over a broad list of tools.
- Do not invent metrics, company interest, product familiarity, or domain motivation.
- Proofread carefully before generating final artifacts.

## Questions Before Drafting

Ask these before writing a new cover letter:

- What genuinely interests you about this company, product, mission, or role?
- Do you have a personal connection to the company, industry, product, customer base, or problem space?
- Which experience should be the lead proof point: applied AI, full-stack software, data engineering, cloud, security, healthcare-adjacent software, insurance data, or research?
- What should the hiring manager remember about you after reading the letter?
- Should the tone be more warm/personal, technical/direct, mission-driven, or startup-style?

## Persona + Technical Structure

Use this structure by default:

1. Opening paragraph: state the role, give the real reason the company or problem matters, and summarize the strongest fit in one sentence.
2. Technical proof paragraph: show one relevant project or role in depth, naming the systems, tools, data, architecture, or workflow involved.
3. Personal/professional fit paragraph: connect Aryan's broader pattern of work to the company: applied AI, useful software, secure workflows, data systems, or tools that make complex work easier.
4. Closing paragraph: reiterate fit and invite a conversation without sounding generic.

## Technical Story Angles

Choose one primary angle per letter:

- Applied AI: LLM-powered applications, RAG/search systems, AI-assisted engineering workflows, prompt/evaluation work, and human-centered AI tools.
- Full-stack software: React, Next.js, Angular, Node.js, Express, PostgreSQL, MongoDB, authentication, RBAC, SSO, JWT, Auth0, Docker, and production-minded workflows.
- Data/cloud engineering: AWS Glue, S3, Lambda, IAM, PySpark, SQL, Impala, Oracle, PostgreSQL, Semarchy MDM, cross-source pipelines, and data standardization.
- Security-aware software: anti-CSRF protection, audit logging, access-denied flows, authentication/authorization, least-privilege access, and cybersecurity education.
- Healthcare/insurance domain: healthcare-adjacent workflow tooling, transplant/logistics support, insurance data standardization, and operational systems used by internal teams.
- Research/education: LLM evaluation, repository mining, cybersecurity labs, ethical hacking tooling, NICE Framework/CAE-CD mapping, and technical teaching.

## Writing Rules

- Start with the company/role context, not a generic self-introduction.
- Use one specific technical example and explain why it matters.
- Keep tool names in service of the story; do not dump a stack list.
- If the company is mission-heavy, connect motivation to the mission and then prove technical fit.
- If the company is engineering-heavy, lead with technical fit and use personal context lightly.
- If the job description repeats specific keywords, incorporate them naturally only when truthful.
- Avoid claims like "perfect fit," "expert," or "passionate about technology" unless the sentence proves it.
- Avoid confidential details, internal names, or unsupported business impact.

## Source Notes

- Yale Office of Career Strategy recommends tailoring each cover letter to a specific job, using confident active language, keeping it to one page, using job-description keywords, and structuring the letter around an opening, middle evidence paragraphs, and a concise close.
- Purdue OWL describes cover letters as a way to explain experience in a story-like format, go deeper on important skills, relate those skills to job requirements, show individualized tailoring, and demonstrate written communication.
